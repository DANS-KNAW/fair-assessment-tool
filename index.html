FAIR-Aware is an online self-assessment tool developed by the FAIRsFAIR project to help you assess your current level of awareness in relation to making your datasets findable, accessible, interoperable and reusable (FAIR) prior to deposit in a data repository for long-term preservation. This tool guides users through the assessment process and aims to raise your overall awareness of the FAIR Principles and to understand how making data FAIR increases the potential value and impact of your data.

This tool is not meant to give you a score for the FAIRness of a specific dataset. You should however have a target dataset in mind to be able to answer the questions and complete the assessment.

The self-assessment tool consists of 19 questions with related guidance and examples. The majority of the questions will help you assess your current levels of awareness about what actions are needed to make data FAIR. There are also a small number of questions to help us better understand the users of the tool, and to help us make FAIR-Aware as user-friendly as possible. You will need approximately 30 minutes to complete the assessment.

Selected datasets should be assigned a globally unique and persistent identifier (PID) so they can be located unambiguously by humans or machines on the web. Persistent identifiers are maintained and governed so that they remain stable and direct the users to the same relevant object consistently over time. Examples of PIDs include Digital Object Identifier (DOI), the Handle System, identifiers.org, w3id.org and Archival Resource Key (ARK).

Identifiers are normally assigned by data repositories (or other service providers) when data and/or metadata are made available through their services. Repositories ensure that the identifier continues to point to the same data or metadata, according to the specified access terms and conditions.

It is worth noting here that not all data you produce during your research will need a PID. In general, those that underpin published findings or have longer term value are worth assigning a PID. If in doubt about which data should be allocated a PID, speak to your local research data management support team.

For example, the DOI system guarantees the persistence (and uniqueness) of its identifiers in two main ways: 1) by applying policies that govern how organisations assign these identifiers, and 2) by operating the necessary technical infrastructures to ensure they continue to point to the same data or metadata, according to specified access terms and conditions (this is called ‘resolving’ the identifier)

Persistent identifiers may point to a data file, a web service response that contains data values, or ideally to an online page that contains metadata for context and the link to access the actual data or details about how to request access). The technical process of translating the identifier to a location is called ‘resolving’ an identifier.

The necessary discovery metadata is descriptive information about the data object (e.g. creator, title, publisher, creation and publication date, summary and keywords describing the data) provided by the data creator when documenting the dataset and/or provided by the depositor in a data repository.

Since the metadata required depends on the type of users and the intended applications, the focus here is on ensuring the minimum descriptive information needed to enable data discovery and citation by potential users - even those from other domains. The metadata should include the PID for the data so that users can discover and access the data. In cases where the data cannot be shared openly for ethical or commercial reasons, the metadata should make clear how a potential reuser can request legitimate access.

Guidelines on descriptive metadata include common data citation guidelines, (e.g., DataCite, ESIP, and IASSIST), and metadata recommendations for data discovery, (e.g., EOSC Datasets Minimum Information (EDMI), DataCite Metadata Schema, and W3C Recommendation Data on the Web Best Practices).

FAIR data also needs the richer more domain-specific metadata that follows a community-endorsed standard. Domain or discipline-specific metadata specifications are covered under a separate assessment question.

The good news is that when you deposit your data with a domain or discipline-specific repository it will likely be using common metadata standards. This means that when you enter the basic details about your research on deposit, your description will conform to standards that enable search and retrieval by users within your domain and potentially beyond your domain. It can pay to spend a bit more time on providing a good description rather than just providing the minimum information required to ensure that your data is not only findable, but can be clearly understood as well.

This question refers to the capability to make the metadata accessible online in a standard and machine-readable format. Machine-readability refers to a structured format that can be read and processed by a computer. By ensuring that the metadata describing your data deposit is machine-readable, your data deposit will be visible to search engines and aggregators which can improve your chances of having your data cited and reused. This capability is normally offered by the data repository that holds the data and makes the essential details about it available online. For example, some repositories expose the metadata online to allow access or harvesting through a standard protocol (e.g. OAI-PMH) and/or a web service. Your choice of data repository will be critical to meet this requirement. You can select a repository offering such capability by searching for example in the Registry of Research Repositories re3data.org for repositories supporting OAI-PMH.

Metadata may also be exposed as structured data embedded within a web page to make it more machine-actionable. The schema.org standard is one approach that helps to ensure indexing by web search engines such as Google and Bing. This allows the Google Dataset Search which you can use to check if data hosted by the repository of your choice is indexed. Dublin Core is another metadata standard that repositories use to make metadata harvestable by indexing systems.

Ideally, datasets should be public domain and openly accessible without restrictions. However, there can be legitimate reasons not to share data such as those due to ethical and commercial constraints. As such, it is up to the depositing researcher to be aware of what can be shared, with whom and when and to take appropriate steps to ensure that the data is as open as possible and as closed as necessary.

The metadata describing your dataset should include details about who can access the data. Access levels can be public, embargoed, restricted, or closed access. In some cases, you may even need to apply a variety of access levels to different parts of the same dataset. It is important to consider these issues early on in your project so that by the time of deposit in a repository, access levels have been clearly defined for any data you will deposit.

Embargoed access refers to data that will be made publicly accessible at a specific date which should be specified in the metadata. For example, a data author may release their data after having published their findings from the data.

Restricted access refers to data that can be accessed under certain conditions (e.g. because of commercial, sensitive, or other confidentiality reasons or the data are only accessible via a subscription or a fee). Restricted data may be available to a particular group of users or after permission is granted. For restricted data, the metadata should include the conditions of access to the data (e.g., point of contact or instructions to access the data). In cases where access will need to be restricted, you should check that the repository offers the required functionality to support access requests before depositing with them.

Closed access refers to data that is not made publicly available and for which only metadata is publicly available.

This question is about whether the metadata will be preserved even when the data they describe may no longer be available. Continued access to data and metadata depends on the data repository preservation practices which are usually documented in the repository’s service policies or statements(e.g. in a preservation plan or policy).

Trustworthy data repositories typically guarantee a minimum retention period. When assigning globally unique and persistent identifiers such as DOIs, trustworthy data repositories ensure that metadata will remain accessible even if the data is no longer available by providing a tombstone page displaying metadata about the removed item.

The preservation of data and metadata is normally considered during the data management planning stage.

If your research data is associated with consent forms (for e.g. personal data) outlining terms and conditions, the data you deposit with a repository may only be legally preserved for a set period of time (e.g., five years after publication) and then must be destroyed. In such cases, the metadata record should make clear that the data no longer exists and why it is not available. However, the metadata record itself should be maintained to provide a record about the research that took place.

A good tip is to consider whether you should include blanket statements about data destruction in the consent forms you use. Speak to your local research data management support and ethics teams early on in your project to make sure you can develop consent forms that match your aims for longer-term data retention and sharing. If eventual data destruction is unavoidable, you will need to check that this can be handled by your choice of data repository.

Metadata should use semantic vocabularies so that the contents are unambiguous and can be interpreted automatically by machines. Using vocabularies in metadata may facilitate enhanced data search and interoperability of data from different sources.

Ontologies, thesauri, and taxonomies are kinds of semantic vocabularies, and they come with different degrees of sophistication (for e.g. in their level of). When semantic vocabularies are included in the metadata, your data repository of choice may be able to publish the metadata in machine-interpretable format, thus greatly increasing their machine actionability.

Linked Data
Best Practices for Publishing Linked Data

Linking data to other kinds of research output will increase its potential for reuse. A data object may be linked to its prior version, other relevant datasets, related publications, data source (for e.g. the instrument or sample), data creators or collectors and organization such as a funder or a host institution.

The linking information – ideally a PID (hover info) – should be captured as part of the metadata. Links between data and its related entities should be explicit and meaningful, and expressed through relation types (for e.g. the DataCite Metadata Schema specifies relation types between research objects through the fields RelatedIdentifier and RelationType).

Content descriptions should be specified in the metadata and they should match with the actual dataset. Examples of content descriptions are resource type (e.g., dataset or collection), place(s), variable(s) measured, code(s), method(s), data format(s), and size(s).

Data should be clearly licenced so that other users can legally reuse it. We encourage the application of licences for all kinds of data whether public, restricted or for specific users. Without an explicit licence, users do not have a clear sense of what can be done with your data. Licences can be of standard type (e.g., Creative Commons) or bespoke licences, and rights statements which indicate the conditions under which data can be reused.

It is highly recommended to use a standard, machine-readable licence such that it can be interpreted by machines and humans. In order to inform users about what rights they have to use a dataset, the licence information should be specified as part of the dataset’s metadata.

By providing provenance information about the data (e.g. sources, date, contributor, version), you enable its (re)use. Data provenance (also known as lineage) represents its history, including people, entities, and processes involved in the data creation. It depends on the data type (e.g., measurement, observation, derived data, or data product) and research domains. For that reason, it is difficult to capture a set of finite provenance records adequate to all domains. Based on existing work, we suggest that the following provenance properties of data generation or collection should be supplied as part of the metadata (this is not an exhaustive list):

  • Sources of data generation or collection, e.g., model, instrument
  • Data creation or collection date
  • Contributor involved
  • Data versioning information

In addition to core metadata required to support data citation and discovery, metadata to support data reusability should be made available following community-endorsed metadata standards.

Some communities have well-established metadata standards (e.g., geospatial: ISO19115, biodiversity: DarwinCore, ABCD, EML, social science: DDI, astronomy: International Virtual Observatory Alliance Technical Specifications) and well-established domain and discipline-specific repositories supporting these community endorsed metadata standards. Community-endorsed metadata standards are listed on registries such as the RDA or Digital Curation Centre Metadata Directories. Domain and discipline-specific repositories should therefore be the preferred choice to deposit your data. The Registry of Research data repositories re3data.org enables searching for a suitable data repository by domain.

In contrast, other communities, including new domains, may have limited standards or standards that are under development (for e.g. engineering and linguistics), and no dedicated data repositories. In such cases the best approach is to contact a research data management expert to identify possible solutions.

File formats refer to methods for encoding digital information. For example, CSV for tabular data, NetCDF for multidimensional data and GeoTIFF for raster imagery.

Data should be made available in a recommended file format that is accepted by the research community to enable data sharing and reuse, and by the repository to enable long-term preservation. See examples of recommended file formats based on data types here.

Recommended formats are widely used and supported by the most common software and tools ensuring that your data can be read in the future, but they will also help increase the reusability and interoperability. Using preferred formats enables data to be loaded directly into the software and tools used for data analysis. It makes it possible to easily integrate your data with other data using the same format. The use of preferred formats will allow migration of the format to a newer one, in case a preferred format becomes outdated.
